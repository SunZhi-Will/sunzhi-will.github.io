Chip giant **Nvidia** is also highly active. They are closely collaborating with memory manufacturers **Samsung** and **SK hynix** to advance the next-generation **High Bandwidth Memory (HBM4E)** technology. HBM is like the "high-speed channel" for AI chips to communicate with the outside world; the greater the bandwidth, the faster AI processes data. This collaboration is undoubtedly to meet the ever-growing demand for computing power and data throughput from future AI models. NVIDIA's advancements in chip technology have always been a focus of ours, for example, the previous GTC conference revealed many new chip eras: [【AI Daily】AI Brains Get a Full Upgrade! However, the challenges brought by AI are also becoming increasingly apparent. The UK's **Society of Authors** recently launched a "**Human Authored**" label, encouraging creators to mark their works as being created by humans, not AI. Behind this initiative is a deep concern over **AI training using copyrighted content**. Many creators are calling for tech companies to pay **licensing fees** for copyrighted works used to train their AI models. This dispute over the ownership and remuneration of **generative AI** content is bound to be a major topic in AI ethics and law in the coming years. This also reminds us once again that AI ethics and regulatory issues are urgent, just like the AI ethical red lines discussed previously: [【AI Daily】AI Gold Rush's Dual Storm: Giants Pour Billions, While Minnows Struggle with Ethical Red Lines?](/blog/2026-03-12-012747).

Finally, despite the rapid development of AI models, research indicates that **AI coding tools** still have an error rate of up to **one quarter**. This means that while AI can assist developers in writing code, its reliability still needs improvement, and the importance of **human oversight** cannot be underestimated. Just as even the smartest autonomous driving system needs a human driver to take over at critical moments, AI tools currently cannot completely replace human precise judgment and review.

### What Does This Mean for Us?

These seemingly distant AI news stories are quietly changing our lives and work patterns.

For me personally, the development of **personalized intelligence** makes me both excited and a little concerned. I'm excited that my digital life will become smoother, with AI filtering out useless information and directly providing the answers I need, like a super-attentive secretary. But I'm concerned that as AI understands me more, will my privacy boundaries become increasingly blurred? This is probably the price for enjoying such convenient services.

For creators, the issue of **AI content copyright** is even more directly relevant. If AI can mimic your style, or even generate works that are indistinguishable from yours, how will your creative value be reflected? The "Human Authored" label is a preliminary attempt, but deeper copyright regulations and business model adjustments will undoubtedly affect every creative industry professional. I believe this will force creators to think about their unique value and how to collaborate with AI rather than be replaced.

The rapid advancement of AI infrastructure and the development of AI coding tools will also have a huge impact on our daily work processes. Imagine that data analysis or code writing that used to take hours or even days could be completed with AI assistance in minutes in the future. This doesn't mean we'll be out of jobs, but rather that our role will shift from executors to "supervisors" and "optimizers," ensuring that AI's output is correct and meets requirements.

AI is a powerful assistant, but the decision-making power and ultimate responsibility currently still rest with humans.
