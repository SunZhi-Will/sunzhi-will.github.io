## The Bottom Line

![AI Won't Replace You — But It Will Replace Someone Who Only Follows Steps](/blog/2026-06-16-ai-judgement/image-1.png)

The more I use AI, the less I see it as a mere "productivity tool."

It's more like a very smart, very diligent intern — capable of getting things done, but occasionally a little too clever for its own good.

You ask it to organize data, it can.
You ask it to write an article, it can.
You ask it to fix code, it really can.

The problem is: **what it produces isn't always what you actually need.**

That's why I wanted to write this.

AI won't directly replace you.
But it will replace the version of you that just follows procedures, copies answers, and feels relieved as long as something — anything — gets produced.

## I First Felt This Way Because of Cursor

The first time I truly felt AI would change how we work wasn't when it helped me write an article, or when it summarized documents for me.

It was when I started writing code with **Cursor**.

Before, building a feature meant finding files myself, understanding the architecture, tracing data flow, fixing logic, debugging errors. It felt like being alone in a kitchen — chopping vegetables, boiling soup, washing dishes — doing everything yourself.

But after Cursor, it was like having a diligent assistant beside you.

You'd say:

> "Help me fix this feature."

And it would start reading the codebase, finding relevant files, proposing changes — even filling in those tedious details you'd rather skip.

At first, it felt amazing.

But after a while, I started noticing something else:

> AI doesn't just make you stronger.
> It also makes it easier to cut corners — in a way that's hard to notice.

Before, when you cut corners, you knew it.
Now, AI produces something that looks complete — and you might even convince yourself you did a great job.

That's the part I find genuinely dangerous.

## The Real Problem Isn't That AI Gets It Wrong — It's That It Looks Too Right

If AI were obviously wrong every time, it'd be easy to handle.

Like an intern who writes terrible reports — you know immediately you can't submit them.

But today's AI is trickier.

It doesn't usually write nonsense. It takes an answer that hasn't been fully thought through and wraps it in a beautiful package.

What you get:

* Clean formatting
* Confident tone
* Complete headings
* Expert-like language
* Phrases that sound like "best practices"

Seeing this, it's easy to let your guard down.

It's like walking into a restaurant where the waiter brings out a beautifully plated dish — herbs, sauce, wooden board, perfect lighting.

Your first thought:

> "This must be delicious."

Then you take a bite and realize it's undercooked.

AI answers can be like that sometimes.

It's not necessarily wrong.
But it might just be serving you something half-baked on a beautiful plate.

If you only look at the surface, it's easy to mistake it for something ready to publish, deliver, or ship.

![The real question isn't "can you use AI" — it's "can you tell if the answer is usable?"](/blog/2026-06-16-ai-judgement/image-2.png)

## I Asked AI to Merge Four Projects — It Built a Food Court

Here's a classic example from my own experience.

I wanted to merge the features of four separate projects — A, B, C, and D.

My mental model was:

* Extract the useful features
* Remove the overlapping parts
* Restructure the architecture
* Turn it into a clean new product

In cooking terms, what I wanted was:

> Open a new restaurant,
> and redesign the signature dishes from four different restaurants into one cohesive menu.

But the instruction I gave AI was too vague.

I roughly said:

> "Help me merge these features together."

AI was very diligent.

It produced something that looked like a finished merge.

But what really happened:

* Project A was still running on its own
* Project B was still running on its own
* Project C was still running on its own
* Project D was still running on its own
* And then AI built a separate page to let me switch between them

In other words, what I wanted was an "integrated new product."
What I got was:

> Four restaurants staying open,
> with a reception desk at the entrance.

That's not a new restaurant.

That's a food court.

And to make things more entertaining, my computer started getting busy.

A frontend service. A backend service. Another background service. My terminal looked like a night market.

The fan noise felt like it was telling me:

> "You weren't building a product —
> you were summoning things."

## What This Really Taught Me

That experience made me realize something important:

AI isn't not working.
It really does work.

But it works on the "literal task" as it understands it — not the "outcome" I actually want.

Those two are very different.

| What I Wanted | What AI Built |
| --- | --- |
| A single integrated product | Four projects running simultaneously |
| A redesigned architecture | A page to switch between them |
| Remove duplicate functionality | Keep everything |
| A cleaner system | My computer running more things |

Without judgment, I might have thought it was done.

I might have kept building on top of it, making the project messier and messier.

This is the trap of the AI era:

> It's not that AI can't produce results.
> It produces results that *look* finished — just finished enough for you to almost believe.

## Knowing How to Ask AI Is the Entry, Not the Advantage

![Common misconception: knowing how to ask AI = having an edge](/blog/2026-06-16-ai-judgement/image-4.png)

A lot of people treat "knowing how to talk to AI" as a superpower.

As if knowing how to write prompts, use Cursor, use Gemini, use Claude Code means you already have a competitive edge in the AI era.

I think we should take a step back here.

Knowing how to ask AI is important.
But it's the entry ticket, not the destination.

It's like knowing how to use a food delivery app doesn't make you a chef.

You can place an order, but it doesn't mean you know which restaurant is actually good.
You can have food brought to your door, but that doesn't mean you can run a kitchen.

The real difference starts after AI gives you the answer.

Some people copy-paste immediately — like opening a delivery bag and serving it without even looking.

Others check first:

* Is this what I need?
* Is the content correct?
* Is the tone appropriate?
* Will this cause problems if I use it as-is?
* Is there a better approach?

It's the same at work.

AI can help you write copy.
But you need to know if it fits your brand.

AI can help you write code.
But you need to know if the architecture will explode later.

AI can help you draft a plan.
But you need to know whether it actually solves the problem — or just wraps it in something that looks good.

## Three Simple Questions to Ask Before Using AI's Output

![Three simple questions to ask before using AI's output](/blog/2026-06-16-ai-judgement/image-5.png)

I'm not saying every AI interaction needs to be a formal review.

You don't need to interrogate AI like a suspect:

> "Do you have evidence for this claim?"
> "Will this architecture scale?"
> "Are you making things up again?"

That's exhausting and impractical.

AI is a tool. Its value is in helping us accelerate, organize, and produce first drafts.

But there are three questions worth turning into habits.

### 1. Is this true?

AI is very good at sounding professional.

But remember:

> **Fluency is not accuracy.**

Writing that flows well doesn't mean the facts are real.
Logic that seems sound doesn't mean the premises are correct.
A professional tone doesn't mean it isn't making things up.

This is especially important for:

* Data and statistics
* Legal information
* Medical advice
* Financial guidance
* News
* Technical documentation
* Product specifications

These things can't just *look* reasonable.
You need to verify.

### 2. Is this appropriate?

A lot of AI answers aren't wrong.

They're just wrong for the context.

You asked for a Threads post, AI gave you an annual shareholder letter.
You wanted to write for non-technical readers, AI gave you a research paper.
You just needed a friendly product description, AI gave you a government RFP document.

That doesn't mean AI is bad.

It means AI doesn't know your context.

It doesn't know who your audience is.
It doesn't know your tone.
It doesn't know your brand.
It doesn't know your readers might have a three-second attention span.

So you need to judge:

> Is this answer right for this platform, this audience, and this purpose?

### 3. Is this risky?

Risk isn't always legal liability.

Sometimes it's:

* Overpromising
* Wrong citations
* Odd tone
* Logic leaps
* Building features in the wrong direction
* Architecture that will be painful to maintain

The four-project merge I mentioned earlier is a perfect example.

On the surface, it looked like the features were there.
In reality, future maintenance would be a disaster.

AI can help you move faster.

But it won't take the blame for you.

When things go wrong, AI won't step up and say:

> "Sorry, I made a mess of that merge."

It'll be you, staring at your screen, saying:

> "Why is my entire computer running right now?"

## Same AI Tool, Very Different Outcomes

![Same AI tool, very different outcomes: some get faster, some get stronger](/blog/2026-06-16-ai-judgement/image-6.png)

The most interesting thing about AI is this:

The same tool, used by different people, produces wildly different results.

Some people use AI to think less.

They say:

* Write this for me
* Do this for me
* Organize this for me
* Complete this for me

Whatever AI gives them, they paste.

Yes, this is faster.

But it might just make you a higher-speed copy-paste machine.

To put it kindly:

> Efficiency went up. Soul went down.

Other people use AI to think more clearly.

They let AI produce a first draft, then review what feels off, then ask why, then request alternatives, then rewrite, and finally make their own judgment.

This approach isn't always the fastest.

But it makes you stronger.

Because you're not treating AI as a substitute.

You're treating it as a sparring partner.

> A substitute can win you one match.
> A sparring partner makes you genuinely better.

## What You Really Need Isn't More Tools — It's a Judgment Habit

![What you really need isn't more tools — it's a judgment habit](/blog/2026-06-16-ai-judgement/image-7.png)

Many people learning AI fall into tool anxiety.

Today Cursor is amazing.
Tomorrow Claude Code is incredible.
The day after, OpenCode seems promising.
Then someone recommends Gemini, Agents, MCP, a new model.

Every tool seems essential.

Every one feels like you're falling behind if you don't learn it.

The result: your tool collection grows, but the number of tools you consistently use well stays small.

It's a bit like buying fitness equipment.

You buy dumbbells, a yoga mat, resistance bands, an ab roller. You look at them every day and feel closer to having abs.

But if you're still on the couch, all that expensive equipment is just home decor.

AI tools are the same.

Tools matter.
But tools don't automatically make you better.

What makes you better is your usage habits.

## A Simple AI Judgment Framework

Every time AI gives you something, run through these four steps:

| Step | Question to Ask Yourself |
| --- | --- |
| Check the source | Where does this come from? Could it be outdated? |
| Check the logic | Is it internally consistent? Or does it just *sound* smooth? |
| Consider the context | Who is this for? Does the tone, platform, and purpose align? |
| Evaluate the risk | Will this create problems later? Will it be hard to maintain? |

These steps don't sound cool.

But they're incredibly useful.

Because the scariest thing about the AI era isn't slow output.

It's fast output.

So fast that before you've fully thought it through, AI has already built you a beautiful building — built on a cracked foundation.

## AI Isn't a Magic Pill — But It's a Powerful Amplifier

I'm not saying AI is bad.

I use it heavily myself, and I genuinely enjoy exploring Vibe Coding, AI agents, and building products with AI.

AI is great for:

* First drafts
* Generating direction
* Comparing options
* Filling in ideas
* Checking blind spots
* Accelerating development
* Lowering the barrier to starting

Before, you might stare at a blank page for thirty minutes.

Now, AI can throw a first version at you — giving you something to discuss and refine.

That's genuinely good.

But AI shouldn't replace your final judgment.

Especially when it comes to:

* Product direction
* Brand voice
* Customer trust
* Technical architecture
* Data accuracy
* Long-term maintenance costs

In these areas, you can't just check whether AI *produced* something.

You need to check whether it produced the *right* thing.

## How I See AI Now

I don't really think of AI as a monster that will replace me anymore.

I also don't treat it as a god I can fully trust.

I see it more like:

> A very smart, very diligent assistant
> who occasionally gets a little too clever.

It can help with a lot.

But you can't just blindly submit what it gives you.

You have to review.
You have to revise.
You have to judge.
And at the end of the day, you're still responsible.

AI can sit in the passenger seat.

But you probably shouldn't hand over the wheel completely.

Especially when you're not sure where you're going yourself — just because the passenger is great at talking doesn't mean you should let them drive all the way.

## Final Thought: What Matters Is Whether You're Still Present

AI won't directly replace you.

But it will replace the version of you that just follows procedures.

It will replace the you that feels relieved as long as there's an answer — that trusts format, that considers something done when it "runs."

But if you hold onto your judgment, AI becomes a powerful amplifier instead.

Have ideas? AI amplifies your ideas.
Have taste? AI amplifies your taste.
Have judgment? AI amplifies your judgment.
Have methods? AI amplifies your methods.

What truly matters isn't whether you know how to use AI.

It's whether, after AI gives you an answer:

> **You're still present.**

Are you still asking:

* Is this true?
* Is this appropriate?
* Is this risky?
* Is this what I want?
* Will this become technical debt in three months?
* Does this content reflect my perspective?

If you're still asking these questions, AI isn't replacing you.

It's helping you amplify your capabilities.
