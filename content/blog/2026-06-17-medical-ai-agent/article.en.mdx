Initially, I thought the hardest part of building a Medical AI Agent was the technology.
But after our conversation, I realized the real key is: **Where do you place the AI?**

Two days ago, I had dinner with a founder who runs a startup building Medical AI Agents.

I had assumed they were building the kind of AI assistant meant directly for end-users.

A user opens the app, inputs their symptoms, the AI analyzes the data, and then recommends medicine, checkups, elder care, or related services.

Sounds reasonable, right?

But a problem immediately came to my mind:

**If the target audience is general consumers—especially middle-aged or elderly people—this product is going to be extremely hard to promote.**

Not everyone knows how to use AI.
Not everyone knows how to describe their health conditions.
And not everyone is willing to pay directly for an AI tool.

Furthermore, decisions regarding medical care, medicine, and senior care aren't as simple as "I will do whatever the AI tells me."

So I asked him:

> "Who is this AI actually for? Is it for general consumers to use directly?"

His reply was very interesting.

He said the tool was never designed for general consumers to use directly in the first place.

Instead, it's for **the people serving them**.

That is, the sales reps, consultants, and service staff. Through the AI, they can understand client situations faster, organize appropriate medicines, services, checkups, and care plans, and then have human staff communicate, judge, and close the deal.

When I heard this, I immediately felt this case study was worth writing down.

Because the truly interesting part isn't "Medical AI is super powerful."

It's that they didn't put the AI in the most difficult place—the one hardest to promote, hardest to educate, and easiest to fail verification.

Instead, they integrated the AI into a workflow that already exists.

## I Thought AI Was for Customers, But the True Users Are Sales Reps

This difference is actually massive.

If the AI is directly exposed to general consumers, the product has to face numerous complex challenges:

| Problem | Why it's troublesome |
| --- | --- |
| Users might not know how to operate it | Especially the elderly, AI adoption barriers are higher than you think |
| Users might not know how to describe needs | Health conditions, family status, and budget are hard to articulate clearly by oneself |
| Users might not trust AI | Medical topics aren't something where "what the AI says goes" |
| Users might not be able to verify results | They may not know if the AI's recommendations are actually reasonable |
| High cost of errors | Medical, drug, and elder care decisions involve real-world risks |

So when he told me this tool is for sales reps and service staff instead of general consumers, I found this direction much more logical.

Because sales reps already need to understand clients.
Consultants already need to organize proposals.
Service staff already need to help communicate.

AI here doesn't replace the human; it helps the human get up to speed much faster.

It can pre-organize data, cross-reference conditions, and list potential solutions—but ultimately, it is still the human who explains, judges, and verifies.

This kind of AI might not look as flashy, but it is much closer to how the real world operates.

## Technical Complexity Isn't Everything, But Right Placement Creates Value

What struck me most is that technically, it doesn't have to be a super complex AI.

You can imagine it doing things like:

* Collecting customer profiles
* Summarizing demand details
* Cross-referencing medicines, checkups, and care options
* Helping sales reps quickly find the best options based on budget, family status, and care requirements

From a purely engineering perspective, some developers might think:

> "Isn't this just data collation and recommendation?"

But the value of an AI product doesn't just depend on its technical difficulty.

It depends on whether it is integrated into a workflow where someone actually uses it, buys it, and can verify the outcomes.

This founder was already in the business of selling related medical products and services.

He didn't just build an AI out of nowhere and then scramble to find customers.

He already knew where the customers were.
He knew what problems they faced.
And crucially, he already had sales and service workflows in place.

AI was simply injected to make the existing process more efficient and precise.

---

### This Matters Greatly to Anyone Building AI Products

Many AI products fail to survive not because the AI isn't powerful enough.

But because they never connected with a real demand in the first place.

The demo is beautiful.
Features are abundant.
The slide deck looks amazing.

But after building it, you realize no one knows why they should use it.

Conversely, some AI applications look quite ordinary, but because they are placed exactly where customers, trust, and transactional scenarios already exist, they actually generate real value.

This doesn't mean technology is unimportant.

But technology without a workflow scenario easily ends up as just a showcase.

## The Biggest Fear for Medical AI Isn't Being Dumb, but Being Placed in the Wrong Role

Let me be very clear here.

I am not saying AI should directly decide what medicine people should take, what treatment they should receive, or what service they should buy.

Medical-related decisions carry high inherent risks.

If AI faces the user directly, it is easily misinterpreted as giving official medical advice.
Users also may not have the expertise to judge if the AI is correct.
If something goes wrong, the problem goes far beyond "a bad tool experience."

Thus, I believe the more reasonable position for Medical AI is a **supporting role**.

It can help organize information, but it cannot replace professional judgment.
It can help service staff understand situations quickly, but it cannot bypass human confirmation.
It can prepare proposals for sales, but the AI's output should never be treated as the absolute final answer.

## Should AI Products Build Tech First or Find Scenarios First?

During our chat, we also talked about a very practical reality.

Many people build extremely cool AI, but have no client acquisition channels.

Other applications are simple—even common—yet because they target a scenario with clear demand, sales channels, and existing trust relationships, they monetize much easier.

This is a bit brutal, but very real.

When building AI products, we shouldn't just ask:

* Is this model strong enough?
* Can this Agent run fully autonomously?
* Does the demo look flashy?

We should ask:

* Whose time does this save?
* Is it placed in a workflow where someone is genuinely willing to pay?
* Does the user have the ability to verify the AI's output?
* When the AI makes a mistake, is there a human who can take over and make the judgment call?

If these questions have no answers, adding more features will likely only make things more chaotic.

When I look at AI tools now, I care less about how many features they have.

I care more about whether they can fit into daily workflows.

If an AI tool seems powerful but I don't know when to use it, or how to verify its output, it remains a toy to me.

But if a tool simply handles a very clear step in my workflow—such as summarizing data, generating drafts, comparing options, or checking specs—even if it's not flashy, it is incredibly useful.

## From Medical AI to Robotics, the Core Issue Is the Same

Later, we also talked about robotics.

They asked me a question:

> "If we want to train a robot, can it just learn by watching tons of videos?"

My immediate reaction wasn't a technical answer, but a counter-question:

> "What do you want the robot to do?"

Because this question comes way before "how to train it."

If you want a robot for elderly care, what exactly is it supposed to do?

| Care Robot Task | Required Capabilities May Vary |
| --- | --- |
| Companionship / Chat | Voice interaction, emotion understanding, long-term memory |
| Medicine Reminder | Scheduling, notifications, identity verification |
| Fall Detection | Sensors, image recognition, instant alerts |
| Fetching Items | Robotic arm manipulation, path planning, object recognition |
| Mobility Assistance | Biomechanics, safety guardrails, body support |
| Home Care Assistance | Task decomposition, environment mapping, error handling |

Each goal requires entirely different capabilities.

You can't just say, "I want to build an elderly care robot."

That is too broad.

If you try to do everything, you will likely end up doing nothing well.

I'm not sure if this view is entirely correct, but this is how I currently understand it:

> Robotics shouldn't start with "I want to build a highly capable robot."
> It should start with "What task does it need to complete?"

The task determines the data.
The data determines the training method.
The training method determines the hardware.
And the hardware, in turn, limits what the robot can do.

## AI Agents Are the Same: Ask Who It Helps and What It Does, Not Just What It Can Do

This logic applies directly to AI Agents.

Today, when people hear "Agent," they immediately want to build a highly intelligent, fully autonomous system that handles everything.

But a healthier question isn't:

> "Should I build an Agent?"

Instead, we should ask:

> "Who is it helping, and what task is it completing?"

It could be:

* Helping sales organize client profiles
* Helping consultants compare proposals
* Helping customer support organize FAQ answers
* Helping course instructors find where students get stuck
* Helping engineers check for missing specifications in PRDs

The clearer the task, the easier it is for the AI to land.

The vaguer the task, the more likely the AI is to remain a showcase piece.

This is why I believe AI products don't need to start with the most complex scope.

Sometimes, if you grab a small, clear, and high-frequency workflow, you already have a chance to build something truly valuable.

The hard part isn't connecting the AI APIs.

It is knowing where exactly it should be placed.

## My Biggest Takeaway from This Chat

Our dinner conversation on June 15 was quite broad.

We went from Medical AI Agents to sales workflows, to elderly care, and finally to robotics.

But looking back, everything points to the same truth:

> **AI applications don't need to be complex; the key is whether they are placed in the right position.**

If you make AI a direct tool for everyone, you have to face:

* High education costs
* Payment barriers
* Trust issues
* User habit adjustments
* Lack of output verification capability

But if you place AI inside an existing business workflow, it is much easier to create and capture value.

Medicine, elderly care, sales, health checkups, home care visits, equipment, reservations—each of these is a massive arena.

It might start with simple drug or service recommendations.
Later, it could extend to scheduling health checks, elder care services, home safety setups, home visits, and reservation management.

But it has to start by being placed in the right workflow.

This is the kind of AI application I find worth studying.

Not because its tech is the flashiest.

But because it is closest to real business.

---

## Conclusion: With the Right Placement, Simple AI Can Be Highly Valuable

This conversation helped me reshape a perspective.

Whether an AI product is valuable doesn't just depend on how strong the model is, or how pretty the demo looks.

It depends on whether it answers these practical questions:

* Who does it help?
* Which workflow does it live in?
* What specific problem does it solve?
* Who evaluates its results?
* How does it turn into a service or a business?

Many AI applications look simple, but they become highly valuable when placed in the right spot.

Many AI products look stunning, but if they lack acquisition channels, workflows, or trust relationships, they will likely remain just demos.

So when I look at AI products now, I ask less:

> "Is this AI powerful?"

I ask first:

> "Where is this AI placed?"

Because in the right position, a simple AI can become a high-value service.

In the wrong position, even the most powerful AI will find no users.
