### What Happened?

I've always felt that AI's development speed is like it's rocket-powered, with new breakthroughs emerging daily. ### According to Reports, Details Are as Follows

The U.S. Department of Defense's latest move is substantial. They announced agreements with eight companies: **Amazon Web Services (AWS)**, **Google**, **Microsoft**, **OpenAI**, **SpaceX**, **NVIDIA**, **Reflection AI**, and **Oracle**. These advanced AI technologies will be deployed in the DoD's "Impact Level 6 (IL6)" and "Impact Level 7 (IL7)" network environments. These levels represent the military's highest classification tiers used for the most sensitive military operations. This initiative aims to transform the U.S. military into an "AI-first" fighting force, enhancing data integration, improving warfighter decision-making, and boosting situational awareness.

Notably, this collaboration builds on the **GenAI.mil** platform, launched in **December 2025**. In just five months, over **1.3 million** departmental personnel have used it, generating tens of millions of prompts and deploying hundreds of thousands of agents. These AI tools are reducing many tasks that previously took months to just days. However, another significant AI company, **Anthropic**, was excluded from this partnership due to disagreements with the DoD over "legitimate operational use" clauses for AI in military applications. Nevertheless, **Anthropic's** latest **Mythos** model, with its powerful cybersecurity vulnerability detection capabilities, is still considered a separate national security issue by the DoD.

On the naval front, to accelerate mine-sweeping operations in the Strait of Hormuz, the U.S. Navy signed a **$99.7 million** contract with San Francisco-based AI company **Domino Data Lab**. This program, named **"Accelerated Machine Learning for Maritime Operations (Project AMMO),"** aims to enable underwater unmanned vehicles (UUVs) to identify new types of mines in just days using AI software, rather than the months it previously took. **Domino's** Chief Operating Officer **Thomas Robinson** stated, "Mine clearance used to be a job for ships; now it's becoming a job for AI."

Regarding the strategic布局 of tech giants:

**Meta** completed the acquisition of robot AI startup **Assured Robot Intelligence** on **May 1st**. Although specific financial terms were not disclosed, a **Meta** spokesperson emphasized that the startup is at the "forefront of robot intelligence," with technology designed to enable robots to understand, predict, and adapt to human behavior in complex, dynamic environments. The team's founders, **Lerrel Pinto** and **Xiaolong Wang**, will join **Meta's** Superintelligence Labs, focusing on optimizing robot application models. Additionally, **Meta** raised its **2026** capital expenditure forecast by **$10 billion**, to **$125 billion to $145 billion**, to account for higher component prices and additional AI data center costs. **Meta's** goal is to become the "Android" of humanoid robots, providing the intelligent foundation for other companies to build hardware.

The expansion of the multi-year strategic partnership between **OpenAI** and **AWS** also marks a significant shift in the AI cloud services market. The de facto exclusive partnership between **Microsoft** and **OpenAI** has come to an end. **Amazon** made a **$50 billion** investment in **OpenAI** in **February**, and **AWS** has become the exclusive third-party cloud distributor for **OpenAI's Frontier** models. **OpenAI** has committed to using approximately **2 gigawatts (GW)** of **Trainium** computing power on **AWS**. This agreement allows **OpenAI's** models to be available not only on **AWS** but potentially also on **Google Cloud** in the future, significantly increasing enterprises' flexibility in deploying generative AI applications and autonomous agent systems.

Regarding social adoption and trust, a new **2026 Stanford HAI AI Index Report** reveals an interesting phenomenon. In Australia, nearly **50%** of people regularly use generative AI, but only **36%** report trusting the technology. This "high adoption, low trust" situation makes Australia a leading nation of "skeptical adopters" globally. This reminds me of a **Humanova** report stating that although **70%** of Australian office workers use AI weekly, only one in eight are "fluent," and executives' AI fluency is **5 times** that of regular employees. This indicates a widespread AI "competency gap" in the workplace.

Financial system regulators are also actively addressing the challenges posed by AI. Federal Reserve Vice Chair **Michelle Bowman** emphasized that regulators need to adopt flexible measures to support banks in safely, effectively, and efficiently implementing AI tools. The Federal Reserve, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) recently revised their model risk management guidelines, explicitly stating that they **do not apply** to generative or agentic AI, and plan to issue a Request for Information (RFI) soon regarding AI applications in the financial system. Quebec's Autorité des marchés financiers (AMF) in Canada also published guidelines for financial institutions using AI, which will come into effect on **May 1, 2027**, requiring financial institutions to establish a centralized directory of AI systems and conduct risk ratings. All this points to the need for more rigorous risk management and regulatory frameworks for AI applications in the financial sector.

Finally, ethical and privacy issues surrounding technologies like **DeepNude AI** remain a focus of public concern. The capability to generate non-consensual images raises serious concerns about individual privacy, online harassment, and abuse. Many governments, including Australia, the U.S., the UK, Canada, and New Zealand, have issued guidelines for the safe deployment of "agentic AI systems," warning against granting AI agents broad or unrestricted access, particularly to sensitive data or critical systems.

### What Does This Mean for Us?

For ordinary people like you and me, the widespread application of AI in the military may sound distant, but it actually affects global peace and stability. When AI enables faster and more precise military decisions, we hope it can reduce conflicts, but at the same time, we must worry about the potential risks of its misuse. Just like in movies, if intelligent weapons lose control, the consequences would be unimaginable.

Trends like **Meta's** active investment in humanoid robots and the proliferation of **OpenAI** models on more cloud platforms are closer to our daily lives. In the future, a smarter, more adaptable robot butler at home might no longer be a dream. But this also raises new questions: Will these robots understand us better? How will their data privacy be protected? These are not only challenges for tech giants but also issues that each of us needs to consider.

Just as we store valuables in a safe deposit box, we need to ensure our personal data has the most stringent protection mechanisms to prevent improper use.

The Australian report also rang an alarm bell for us: while we enjoy the convenience brought by AI, we don't fully trust it internally. This is like enjoying the convenience of food delivery but always having some doubts about food safety. This "skeptical adopter" psychology shows that for AI to truly integrate into society, it not only needs powerful technology but also a solid foundation of trust. This requires companies to be more transparent in AI development and governments to be more comprehensive in regulation to alleviate public concerns.

Changes in AI regulation in the financial sector directly relate to the security of our wallets. If banks can identify fraud more accurately through AI, that's definitely a good thing for us. But if AI exhibits bias in loan approvals or causes financial system instability due to model flaws, the consequences could be very serious. Therefore, regulators' cautious approach is necessary; they are striving to find a balance between innovation and risk.

As for **DeepNude AI**, this presents a very real and severe ethical test. The proliferation of such technologies challenges the trust in "seeing is believing" like never before. As information receivers, we need to cultivate higher discernment and maintain a degree of vigilance toward images and videos seen online. At the same time, governments and tech companies also have a responsibility to curb the misuse of such technologies through both technical and legal means.

Behind today's AI news, I see another major reshuffle of the global power structure and economic landscape. The deep collaboration between the U.S. DoD and top AI companies sends a clear message: whoever masters the commanding heights of AI will control future military initiative. This "AI-first" strategy will further stimulate countries' investment in AI technology, forming a new wave of tech arms races.

OpenAI's shift from an exclusive partnership to a multi-cloud strategy is not just a change in business model; it's a critical step toward the popularization of AI services. In the past, Microsoft's exclusive access to OpenAI's leading technology, while accelerating Azure's development, also limited OpenAI's ability to reach a wider range of customers. Now, OpenAI's embrace of AWS and Google Cloud is akin to opening up the "recipe" for top AI models to more "chefs," allowing more enterprises to innovate using these models. This is a huge boon for the entire AI industry and intensifies competition among cloud service providers, ultimately benefiting a broad spectrum of enterprise users.
