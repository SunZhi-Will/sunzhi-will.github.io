Speaking of money, OpenAI's **$122 billion** private funding round is indeed one of the largest in the history of AI, and even the entire tech sector. On the hardware front, the **$2 billion** AI alliance between **Nvidia** and **Marvell** is focused on the development of **silicon photonics technology**. This technology aims to transmit data using light rather than electrons, significantly boosting communication speed and efficiency between AI chips, which is crucial for training Large Language Models (LLMs) and running complex AI applications. ### Insider's Deep Analysis
From my perspective as a seasoned investigative journalist, behind today's headlines lies a "high-stakes gamble" by tech giants. **OpenAI** securing such a massive sum isn't just a display of capital's enthusiasm; it's an "endorsement" of its future commercial potential. This money will allow them to maintain a lead in the AI arms race, much like flooring the accelerator in a race car. However, massive investments also come with immense pressure for returns; they must prove that AI is not merely a technological breakthrough but can also achieve "profitability" commercially. The lesson from **Sora**'s early exit due to excessive operating costs, as noted in [AI Reshuffle: The Bitter Lesson of Sora's Exit, The Era of "Real Action" with GPT-5, Gemini 3.1, and AI Agents Is Here!](/blog/2026-03-29-015816), also indicates that AI's business models are still being explored.

The collaboration between **Nvidia** and **Marvell**, however, underscores the importance of infrastructure. As the saying goes, "If you want to do a good job, you must first sharpen your tools." AI models are growing ever larger; without top-tier chips and high-speed communication technology, everything is mere talk. Silicon photonics technology is key to resolving current data transmission bottlenecks, and this **$2 billion** investment is essentially "laying the foundation" for the entire AI industry's future.

Meanwhile, active government intervention in AI governance is a very healthy sign. When a technology possesses the immense potential to change society, corporate self-regulation alone is far from sufficient. California's and Australia's actions foreshadow that more countries will introduce more specific AI laws and regulations in the future. This is akin to when the internet first emerged, and everyone was figuring out how to regulate online content and protect personal privacy. AI ethics and safety will become "hard metrics" that tech companies must confront, rather than optional "soft skills."

Finally, AI's impact on the labor market is a long-term societal issue. What I see behind this is a colossal transformation of social structures. We cannot simply view AI as a "replacer"; rather, we should see it as a "collaborator." Businesses need to redesign workflows, individuals need to relearn skills, and governments need to establish more comprehensive social security policies to navigate this transitional period smoothly.
