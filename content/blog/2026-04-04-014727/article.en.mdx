### Simply Put, What Is This?

Imagine your home having an incredibly powerful **"digital brain factory."** This factory needs to feast on huge "electricity meals" and "water resource drinks" to operate. Now, tech companies worldwide are frantically building more and more of these factories, making them bigger and bigger, much like every city competing to build a massive department store. What's the result? Our "public power grids" and "reservoirs" are starting to struggle, and electricity and water bills are climbing. Isn't this exactly the AI infrastructure and energy consumption problem we're facing today?

As for **"AI agents,"** you can think of them as your "digital assistant" upgrading to a "digital butler." Previously, the assistant only acted when you gave instructions. Now, the butler thinks for itself, plans independently, and even carries out complex multi-step tasks without you needing to monitor every step. They are not only changing how we interact with software but also prompting a major transformation in business operations.

Regarding the **"AI chip war,"** this is like everyone used to scramble for imported sports car engines. Now, China can produce high-performance engines themselves, and major clients are lining up to place orders. This isn't just commercial competition; it's also about nations vying for technological autonomy.

In short, AI is no longer a cool gadget in a lab; it's transforming our world in a very real and impactful way.

### According to Reports, Details Are as Follows

First, let's look at **Microsoft**, the "big spender" in AI infrastructure, and how it's "acquiring properties" globally. Recently, Microsoft announced an investment of up to **$10 billion** in Japan over the next four years (2026-2029) to build AI data centers, enhance cybersecurity, and plan to train **1 million** engineers by 2030. When I saw this number, I couldn't help but think: "This is like moving entire AI mansions to Japan!" This investment adds to the **$2.9 billion** allocated in 2024, demonstrating Microsoft's commitment to the Japanese market and the country's growing demand for cloud and AI services.

This initiative will also involve collaboration with Japan's **SoftBank Group** and **Sakura Internet** to expand local technology infrastructure. Interestingly, as soon as the news broke, **Sakura Internet's** stock price immediately surged by **20.2%** – truly an "AI concept stock" magic wand in the stock market!

Beyond Japan, Microsoft's expansion in Southeast Asia is progressing simultaneously. In late March this year, Microsoft announced it would invest over **$1 billion** in Thailand and **$5.5 billion** in Singapore over the next two years, also to expand cloud and AI infrastructure. Microsoft President **Brad Smith** stated that these efforts are to meet the increasing global demand, especially in Asia, for cloud and AI services.

However, the construction of these "digital hearts" also brings significant problems, namely astonishing **energy consumption**. Data centers, these "warehouse-like facilities" that power AI tools (from chatbots to image generators), are sprouting up worldwide like mushrooms after rain. Currently, their electricity consumption is already a concern, with AI data centers projected to consume over **8%** of the United States' electricity by 2030. This makes me think: if my home air conditioner was constantly on, my electricity bill would surely skyrocket, let alone these supercomputers that run non-stop!

Even more troubling is the staggering demand for water resources by AI data centers. Large facilities can consume up to **5 million gallons** of water daily for cooling. Furthermore, research indicates that a typical AI conversation (around 20-50 queries) can consume nearly **500 milliliters** of water, equivalent to a bottle of bottled water. This reminds me of the anxiety during summer water restrictions; if AI keeps "drinking" like this, where will our water come from? In the U.S., over **230** organizations have protested, calling for a moratorium on data center construction because they are depleting local water resources and stressing infrastructure.

</Callout>

Next, let's look at another significant AI trend: the rise of **AI Agents**. These AIs are no longer the passive chatbots that just answered your questions; they can now **autonomously plan, execute multi-step tasks, and even coordinate actions across systems**. This is like your "digital assistant" suddenly transforming into a "highly efficient special assistant" who can handle a multitude of tasks on their own.

In this "AI agent melee," AI giants like **OpenAI** and **Anthropic** are adjusting their strategies. **OpenAI** recently shut down its video generation platform, Sora, due to excessively high operating costs, reallocating resources to develop **agentic AI and coding tools**. This makes me think that even the coolest technology might not survive without a solid business model. Meanwhile, **Anthropic** is internally testing an "always-on AI agent" called **"Conway,"** which can complete multi-step tasks with minimal human intervention. **Microsoft** has also upgraded its Copilot platform, introducing the "Cowork" agent tool, allowing multiple AI models to collaborate and improve output quality.

To enable these AI agents to work together more effectively, a standardized protocol called **"Model Context Protocol (MCP)"** has emerged. You can think of MCP as the "universal language" among AI agents, allowing them to seamlessly connect to various external tools and services, such as databases and Application Programming Interfaces (APIs). This open standard, supported by **Anthropic** and donated to the **Linux Foundation**, has been adopted by major AI platforms including ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Microsoft Copilot. It can reduce integration time from weeks to hours, significantly boosting efficiency. Additionally, Google's Chrome team and Microsoft are jointly developing a browser-native standard called **WebMCP**, enabling AI agents to interact directly with web interfaces.

In terms of **AI chip competition**, developments in the Chinese market are particularly noteworthy. Chinese AI startup **DeepSeek** is reportedly set to fully adopt **Huawei's** independently developed chips, specifically the **Ascend 950PR**, in their latest V4 AI model. This marks a significant shift, symbolizing a firm step forward for China in its pursuit of AI technological autonomy. Reports indicate that Huawei's Ascend 950PR chips can, in some scenarios, offer **2.8 times** the computing power of Nvidia's H20 chips.

My first reaction upon seeing this news was: "Wow, the AI chip market is about to change drastically!" Furthermore, Chinese tech giants like **Alibaba Group, ByteDance, and Tencent Holdings** are rumored to have placed large orders for hundreds of thousands of chips with Huawei, demonstrating the robust development momentum of China's domestic chip industry. Even more intriguing is that DeepSeek did not allow U.S. chip manufacturers early access before the V4 model's release, further emphasizing its reliance on the local supply chain.

Finally, AI applications are becoming increasingly prevalent across various industries, giving rise to the concept of **"AI-native workers."** For instance, in the medical field, multi-agent systems combined with the MCP protocol are being used in **drug discovery**, automatically screening vast amounts of data to accelerate new drug development. In education, Generative AI (GenAI) allows teachers, even without programming backgrounds, to easily design simulation-based interactive courses, truly a "magic wand" for educators. Even the sports world has jumped on this bandwagon, with **Lenovo** partnering with football superstar **David Beckham** to promote AI applications in football, aiming to enhance team performance and fan experience.

For us "workers," AI is redefining our roles. In the future workplace, people will increasingly engage in supervisory and strategic tasks, while AI will become an essential **"digital teammate."** **Salesforce** has also upgraded its Slack platform, adding **30** AI-powered new features and incorporating reusable AI skills, aiming to transform AI from an assistive tool into a coordinating executor, making enterprise workflows smoother.

According to a large-scale study by Anthropic, while **67%** of people hold positive attitudes towards AI, their biggest concern is AI producing **"hallucinations,"** meaning providing inaccurate information, accounting for **27%**. This is followed by worries about job displacement and loss of autonomy, each at **22%**. This makes me think that just as you can't fully trust a verbose but occasionally nonsensical friend, AI's accuracy remains critical. Nevertheless, most people still expect AI to boost productivity and even allow them more time with family. However, scholars also warn that even if AI can fluently use other languages, its inherent **"Western worldview"** might still influence the advice it provides, which requires special attention during use.

### What Does This Mean for Us?

This wave of AI will absolutely have a comprehensive impact on us. From my observations:

*   **Your electricity bill might become more "exciting"**: If the demand for AI data centers continues to grow, our power grids will face greater pressure, and electricity price increases are almost inevitable. This is like everyone speeding on the highway; tolls are bound to go up. We might need to consider supporting the development of renewable energy or choosing AI services that are more energy-efficient.
*   **"Digital butlers" will be ubiquitous**: In the future, your work software, and even smart home appliances, might include AI agents. They will automatically help you schedule appointments, manage emails, and even analyze data, freeing you from repetitive tasks. However, this also tests our **"supervisory capabilities"**; after all, no matter how capable a butler is, they still need their master's guidance, right? Cybersecurity issues will also become more complex because a compromised AI agent could cause immense damage faster and more imperceptibly than a human hacker.
*   **Tech competition enters a "New Cold War"**: China's independent R&D and application in the AI chip sector mean the global tech supply chain will become more diversified, potentially leading to more geopolitical considerations. In the future, countries might lean more towards developing their "sovereign AI" infrastructure to ensure data security and technological independence.
*   **Work models are undergoing a major reshuffle, but the human touch remains essential**: Many repetitive, low-creativity jobs will be replaced by AI. Still, it will simultaneously foster new roles requiring **critical thinking, creativity, and interpersonal interaction**. We need to continuously learn new skills to become "AI directors," not "AI actors." Especially regarding the AI "worldview" issue, it reminds us that no matter how fluent AI is, human culture, emotions, and values remain irreplaceable.

<Callout type="info" title="Become an 'AI Director,' Not an 'AI Actor'">
In the age of AI, we must cultivate supervisory skills, critical thinking, and creativity, viewing AI as a tool, not an end. Only by doing so can we navigate this technological wave and ensure AI truly serves humanity, rather than dominating it.
</Callout>

### Insider's Deep Analysis

From my perspective, this wave of AI investment frenzy is, in essence, an "AI arms race" among tech giants, where no one can afford to lose. Microsoft's massive investments in Japan and Southeast Asia are not just about market expansion; they are about seizing the high ground in **"sovereign AI."** As nations increasingly prioritize data sovereignty and national security, establishing local data centers and training local talent becomes an inevitable strategic choice. This isn't merely "selling services"; it's about "deeply intertwining" with local economies and national strategies.

As for the **energy consumption issue of AI data centers**, I believe this will become one of the biggest bottlenecks for AI development in the coming years. Upgrading power grid infrastructure and finding more efficient, environmentally friendly cooling technologies will be challenges the entire industry must face. Without solutions, AI's development pace will eventually be hampered by "energy costs," potentially even triggering societal controversies. This is like a luxury sports car being flashy, but if the fuel costs too much to drive, it's useless.

Regarding the **rise of AI agents**, this signifies AI's formal transition from the "tool phase" into the "partnership phase." The emergence of open standards like MCP is a signal of industry maturity, enabling AI agents' interoperability and accelerating their adoption in enterprises. I see behind this a pursuit of ultimate efficiency and automation by businesses. But at the same time, this also places higher demands on corporate cybersecurity defenses because an autonomously executing AI agent, if maliciously exploited, possesses enormous destructive potential.
