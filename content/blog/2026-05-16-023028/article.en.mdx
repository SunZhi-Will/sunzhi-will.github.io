### What Happened?

Hey everyone! I'm your tech storyteller, and today I want to chat about the global AI wave. It's not just a trend; it's more like a tsunami, reshaping our lives and quietly transforming the very backbone of global industries. While we're still debating if AI will take our jobs or how smart chatbots are, the world behind the scenes has entered a "free-for-all," with frenzied investments and technological breakthroughs on one side, and deep anxieties about resources, security, and future risks on the other.

Today, specifically, while there isn't one single blockbuster headline, piecing together various reports reveals that AI's development has reached a critical "qualitative shift." We're increasingly hearing discussions about "Sovereign AI" and supply chain "localization," alongside persistent "bubble" warnings. Of course, Taiwan's position in the global AI puzzle is becoming even more indispensable. All of this is happening subtly, but the impact could be far more profound than we imagine.

### Simply Put, What Is This?

Imagine AI as a super capable, yet incredibly "power-hungry" and "data-guzzling" smart baby. This baby has an astonishing appetite from birth, needing vast amounts of electricity to power its "brain" and massive "nutritional data" to learn and grow. So, countries are now figuring out how to "raise" this baby, even hoping it can grow up in their "own home" – this is the concept of "Sovereign AI."

Previously, we might have pictured global factories as one large central kitchen, with everyone collaborating. But now, each country wants to build its own "AI-exclusive" mini-kitchen, controlling everything from ingredients (data), utensils (chips, data centers), to the final dishes (AI applications). They even have to worry about running out of ingredients (data exhaustion) or the kitchen accidentally catching fire (cybersecurity risks). Taiwan, in this scenario, is like the key player holding the most unique "top-tier kitchen utensil" recipe, making it indispensable globally.

### According to Reports, Details Are as Follows

These past two days, I've seen several highly noteworthy details regarding AI's development:

First, Taiwan's position in the global AI supply chain has been reinforced once again. **TSMC**'s leadership in advanced processes and packaging technology has practically etched "indispensable" onto the global AI industry map. Their 2nm chip production is expanding simultaneously across five fabs, and the annual compound growth rate of advanced CoWoS packaging technology is astonishing, projected to exceed **80%** from 2022 to 2027. It's as if the world is building AI super mansions, and TSMC holds the most critical rebar, cement, and decor technology – everyone needs them.

Second, the concept of "Sovereign AI" is evolving from mere "data sovereignty" to "sovereign AI data infrastructure." **Foxconn Chairman Young Liu** explicitly pointed out that this means not only must AI data centers be localized, but the entire related AI supply chain must follow suit. He also revealed that the Taiwan Electrical and Electronic Manufacturers' Association (TEEMA) is promoting a "Science Park 3.0" initiative, planning to establish footprints in Mexico, Poland, the US, and India, with the goal of helping Taiwanese SMEs expand globally and build more resilient supply chains. This is not just a business strategy, but an inevitable choice under global geopolitics, ensuring that no country's AI development is held hostage.

Furthermore, the AI chip market's feverish pace is red-hot, yet it has triggered serious "bubble" warnings. Bank of America strategist **Michael Hartnett** cautioned that the current surge in AI chip stocks is already approaching, or even surpassing, the extreme levels seen before the 2000 dot-com bubble and the 1720 French Mississippi bubble. The price of the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) is even **62%** above its 200-day moving average. While some argue that this time is different from the dot-com bubble because major tech giants are investing with cash flow rather than debt and overvaluation, the fact remains that global AI-related investment is projected to exceed **$2.5 trillion** by 2026, with approximately **50%** flowing into data centers, chips, and network infrastructure. This indicates a definite speculative frenzy in the capital markets.

Simultaneously, concerns about AI safety are becoming increasingly tangible. UK financial regulators (the Treasury, Bank of England, and Financial Conduct Authority) have jointly issued a warning, noting that "the cyber capabilities of current frontier AI models already exceed what skilled human experts can achieve, and do so faster, at a larger scale, and lower cost." This could pose a systemic risk to financial stability. It's like having a super smart AI butler at home; it can manage everything perfectly, but if it turns rogue, the consequences could be disastrous. Internationally, the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) has also released AI risk management guidelines, emphasizing the importance of Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, and and Transparency (FEAT principles).

Moreover, AI's appetite for infrastructure is truly insatiable. OpenAI has even pledged to invest **$1.4 trillion** over the next eight years to build new data centers, aiming to provide **10 gigawatts** of computing power. Just imagine, this is almost equivalent to the output of a small nuclear power plant! This also brings to mind the "2026 problem" we discussed in the past, the concern that AI training data might be exhausted. While new search results don't directly mention data exhaustion, such immense computing demands will undoubtedly exert unprecedented pressure on the sources, storage, and processing of high-quality training data. Without sufficient, high-quality "feed," even the smartest AI baby will struggle to continue growing.

<Callout type="info" title="AI's Challenge to Data Infrastructure">
  As AI models scale up, the demand for high-quality, diverse, and continuously updated training data will grow exponentially. This not only tests data collection and processing capabilities but also involves data sovereignty, privacy protection, and ethical fairness. If data supply cannot keep pace with demand, it may limit further breakthroughs in AI technology.
</Callout>

Of course, the speed of AI application deployment is also beyond imagination. Whether in finance, healthcare, or retail, AI is playing a huge role. AI in the fintech market is projected to reach **$66.5 billion** by 2030, and even more astonishing in the healthcare market, projected to hit **$505.59 billion** by 2033. AI-assisted radiologists can read more scans, financial analysts can track more risk signals, and retailers can make more precise inventory decisions. China's Nanjing and Anhui provinces have also successively released action plans to vigorously develop AI terminal industries and deep applications, targeting industry scales of several hundred billion RMB.

Interestingly, **Transcend Information Chairman Peter Shu** noted in a report that AI has completely shattered the memory industry's 30-year boom-bust cycle, calling it not a cycle, but an "era-defining revolution." This wave of AI-driven memory demand is expected to continue beyond 2028, with no signs of price reversal. Many customers who previously procured directly from original manufacturers are now turning to Taiwanese memory module makers for supply due to shortages, making Taiwanese companies unexpected beneficiaries of this AI wave.

### What Does This Mean for Us?

I believe all of this is deeply relevant to us.

First, for Taiwan, our international standing has become even more important because of AI. As I often say, Taiwan is not just a "tech island," but an "AI core island"! Whether it's TSMC's advanced processes, CoWoS packaging, or Foxconn Chairman Young Liu's strategy to promote "Sovereign AI supply chain localization," Taiwan possesses irreplaceable strategic value in the global AI ecosystem. This means that in the future, more high-end technology and talent will remain in Taiwan or be deeply connected to it, injecting a strong boost into our economy.

Second, we need to start seriously considering the "energy anxiety" brought by AI. When OpenAI plans to build a **10 gigawatt** data center, I don't just think about how much money they need, but how much electricity it will consume. If our "AI baby" gets smarter and hungrier, can our power grid handle it? This is not just the government's responsibility; it's an issue everyone needs to face. After all, we all want an endless supply of electricity to support AI's convenience in our lives, not for AI to "go on strike" due to power shortages.

Third, the "AI bubble" warning in the financial market is a reminder for every investor. When stocks rise too fast, too dramatically, even beyond what fundamentals can explain, we need to be extra cautious. While the AI revolution is real, history shows that even the greatest technological innovations can't escape market overspeculation. This reminds me of the dot-com bubble, where many companies with no substantial business saw their stock prices soar, only to crash overnight. Although the situation is different now, as many large companies have solid cash flows, individual investors, in particular, need to stay calm and avoid blindly following trends.

Finally, AI's "ethics and security" are no longer a future concern, but a present reality. The warnings from UK financial regulators and the proactive efforts of various countries to establish AI governance frameworks all indicate that the potential risks brought by AI are imminent. This includes data privacy, algorithmic bias, and cybersecurity threats from AI systems that could be maliciously exploited. If AI isn't properly governed, it could become an uncontrollable "beast," ultimately harming us.

### Insider's Deep Analysis

From the perspective of a veteran investigative journalist, I believe that AI's development is currently in a stage of "qualitative transformation from quantitative change." In recent years, we've witnessed a frantic stacking of AI computing power and model scale – an "arms race" style of development. But now, with the emergence of the "Sovereign AI" concept and the strong demand for supply chain localization, I see a profound desire among nations for control over AI. This is not just a technological issue; it's a microcosm of national strategy, economic security, and even geopolitics.

> "AI is no longer just a single technological proposition; it is the core engine driving global economic supply chains and industrial upgrading." — **Young Liu** (Foxconn Chairman)

Chairman Liu's statement precisely captures the essence of AI. It's no longer just an application; it's becoming a core infrastructure, like electricity or the internet, that powers the entire society. When a technology reaches this level, its supply chain, security, and how it is governed all ascend to the level of national consideration. Taiwan's ability to ride this wave is precisely due to our deep accumulation in the semiconductor and ICT industries, allowing us to play a core "architect" role in the "new AI world's" infrastructure.

My personal observation is that the "era-defining revolution" in the memory industry is particularly interesting. For decades, the memory market has had its inherent boom-bust cycles, and investors have grown accustomed to these "tides." But **Transcend Chairman Peter Shu** believes that the demand driven by AI is structural; it doesn't have a clear replacement cycle like PCs or smartphones, but will continuously expand. This is like moving from the internal combustion engine era to the electric vehicle era, where a fundamental shift in the power system renders many traditional industry rules obsolete. For Taiwanese memory module manufacturers, this is an excellent opportunity to redefine their role in the global value chain.

However, all immense opportunities come with immense challenges. AI's power demand will be a long-term issue. I believe this will accelerate the development of green energy and energy storage technologies, and even drive investment in advanced energy technologies like nuclear fusion. Because without sufficient, stable, and clean power, AI's development will hit a bottleneck. This also makes me consider that energy supply might become one of the critical factors for national competitiveness in the future.

But importantly, much of the current AI infrastructure investment is driven by tech giants with substantial cash flows. This differs from the dot-com bubble, where many shell companies relied on burning cash. So, rather than a full-scale collapse, the market might undergo an adjustment that "winnows out the chaff," allowing truly capable and technologically sound companies to continue thriving.
