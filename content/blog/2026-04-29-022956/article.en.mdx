#### Theme 1: AI Governance and Ethical Challenges ⚖️

**1. Musk vs. OpenAI: The Battle for Original Principles**
Yesterday, the lawsuit filed by **Elon Musk** against OpenAI officially opened, with the case focusing on whether OpenAI deviated from its initial non-profit mission. **Musk's** lawyer, **Steven Molo**, directly accused OpenAI's leadership in court of "stealing a charity," alleging that **OpenAI CEO Sam Altman** and President **Greg Brockman** transformed the company into a for-profit entity, abandoning the commitment to develop Artificial General Intelligence for humanity's benefit. **Musk** himself testified, claiming that if OpenAI wins, it would set a precedent for "plundering" all charities in the US.

This landmark lawsuit is expected to last approximately three weeks, and its outcome could affect OpenAI's valuation of up to **$1 trillion**.

**2. Google and the US Department of Defense's AI Partnership: Blurring Ethical Lines?**
Also yesterday, **Google** reached a classified agreement with the US Department of Defense, allowing the Pentagon to use its AI systems for "any legitimate government purpose," positioning **Google** among tech giants like OpenAI and xAI collaborating with the US military. This news immediately sparked strong internal backlash within **Google**. Reports indicate that over **600 Google employees** (including members from DeepMind) co-signed a letter to **CEO Sundar Pichai**, urging him to reject the deal due to concerns that AI technology could be used for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons without human oversight.

A **Google** spokesperson emphasized that the company remains committed to its principles that AI should not be used for domestic mass surveillance or autonomous weapons without appropriate human oversight, stating that providing commercial models via API interfaces is a "responsible way to support national security." This collaboration marks a significant shift in **Google's** stance on military AI. In **2018**, **Google** withdrew from the "Project Maven" military AI project due to employee protests. This move is seen as part of **Google's** strategy to reshape its defense sector and compete with **Amazon AWS** and **Microsoft** for government cloud contracts, aiming to add **$6 billion** in contract value by **2027**.

**3. China Blocks Meta's Acquisition of AI Startup Manus: A Geopolitical Chess Match**
In the AI domain, geopolitical influence is becoming increasingly prominent. Yesterday, China's National Development and Reform Commission officially blocked **Meta's** acquisition of the AI startup **Manus**, causing the approximately **$2 billion** deal to collapse. Although **Manus** is headquartered in Singapore, its AI agent technology originated in China, raising concerns in Beijing about the transfer of critical AI technology.

China's move is interpreted as strict control over cross-border AI technology transfers, aiming to prevent national strategic AI assets from falling into foreign hands. Reports suggest that **Manus's** two co-founders were restricted from leaving the country during the review, sending a warning to tech entrepreneurs and investors hoping to circumvent US-China regulations through "Singapore-washing" models. The obstruction of this acquisition not only reflects the escalating competition between the US and China in AI but also introduces more uncertainty for other AI companies seeking international expansion.

#### Theme 2: AI Technological Development and Enterprise Applications 💡

**1. OpenAI Models and Codex Deeply Integrated into Amazon Bedrock: The Fast Lane for Enterprise AI**
For enterprises, the adoption of AI applications is accelerating. Yesterday, OpenAI announced an expanded strategic partnership with **Amazon Web Services (AWS)**, integrating its latest AI models (including **GPT-5.5**) and the powerful code generation tool **Codex** into the **Amazon Bedrock** platform. This means enterprises can now leverage OpenAI's cutting-edge AI tools for development within the familiar AWS environment, while benefiting from AWS's existing security, compliance, and infrastructure advantages.

**Codex** is already used by over **4 million people** weekly, primarily for tasks such as automated code generation, refactoring, and testing. This integration also includes OpenAI-powered **Managed Agents**, capable of handling complex multi-step workflows, allowing enterprises to accelerate the transition of AI from experimental stages to production.

**2. Amazon Quick: Your Desktop AI Assistant Is Live**
Beyond cloud services, personalized AI assistants are increasingly integrated into our daily work. Yesterday, AWS launched its new desktop AI assistant, **Amazon Quick**, designed to break down existing AI tool ecosystem limitations and enable AI to collaborate seamlessly across applications, tools, and data. **Amazon Quick** can help users create presentations, smart dashboards, and connect to various popular applications, thereby improving work efficiency. This is like having a "super secretary" added to your computer, capable of understanding your work habits, data, and processes, making it easier than ever to find information and complete tasks across multiple applications.

**3. Deep Integration of AI in Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)**
Internal enterprise automation processes are also being revolutionized by the addition of AI. According to yesterday's report, AI is driving the most significant transformation in enterprise information systems in 20 years, with nearly **90% of Czech companies** actively using, testing, or planning to implement AI in their ERP systems. AI applications in ERP include predicting cash flow, demand forecasting, risk detection, and using AI agents and assistants in finance, supply chain management, and project management. This signifies that AI is no longer just a single-function tool but is gradually becoming a core driver of enterprise management, compressing corporate decision-making cycles from days to seconds.

#### Theme 3: AI Industry Landscape and Infrastructure 📊

**1. Google DeepMind Enters South Korea: The Global AI Talent Race**
Competition in the AI sector is not just a technological contest but also a race for talent and infrastructure. Yesterday, **Google DeepMind** announced the establishment of its first global AI campus outside the UK, located in Seoul, South Korea, covering approximately **1,980 square meters (about 600 pyeong)**. This "**Google AI Campus**" will serve as a central hub for the "K-Moonshot program," aiming to foster AI technology collaboration between **Google DeepMind** and local Korean startups and researchers in critical areas such as life sciences and climate research.

The South Korean government holds high expectations for this initiative, hoping to attract global AI talent and transform South Korea into a global AI powerhouse. Although **Google** has numerous AI research centers worldwide, this is the first time it has used the "AI Campus" designation, indicating **Google's** strategy for deep-seated deployment in key countries.

**2. DeepSeek Ignites AI Price War: Market Competition Intensifies**
In the AI model market, a fierce price war is underway. Yesterday, Chinese generative AI company **DeepSeek** significantly reduced the price of its latest AI model, **V4**, which is reportedly an astonishing **97% cheaper** than OpenAI's **GPT-5.5**. This aggressive pricing strategy aims to attract more developers and enterprises seeking high-value AI solutions and is expected to disrupt the existing market landscape.

**3. AI Data Centers' "Nuclear Energy Race": Compute Infrastructure Bottlenecks and Innovation**
The explosive growth of AI also poses unprecedented challenges for underlying compute infrastructure. A report yesterday indicated that the Asia-Pacific region (excluding Japan) data center market is projected to grow at an annual rate of **22%** between **2024 and 2029**, primarily driven by surging demand for AI, cloud, and digital services. However, AI workloads demand **7-8 times** more energy consumption than traditional computing, with hyperscale AI data centers consuming over **100 megawatts**, equivalent to the electricity usage of tens of thousands of households.

This makes power supply and grid access critical limitations for deploying AI capabilities, even surpassing the importance of chip supply. To address these challenges, data center operators are actively investing in advanced power infrastructure, more distributed AI deployments, faster energy strategies like on-site power generation, and widespread adoption of digital twin technology and liquid cooling systems. Liquid cooling technology has transitioned from experimental to indispensable, with the data center liquid cooling market projected to surge from **$6.6 billion in 2026** to **$38.4 billion in 2033**, a compound annual growth rate of **28.7%**.
