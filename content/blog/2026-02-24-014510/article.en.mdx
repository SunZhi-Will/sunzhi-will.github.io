### What Happened?

I must say, today's AI news is like a high-octane tech drama! üé¨ First, a headline exploded on Wall Street: tech giant **IBM**'s stock **plunged by 13%** on Monday (February 23rd), marking its largest single-day drop since 2000! ### Simply Put, What Is This?

Let's use an analogy to understand these two events.

First, IBM's stock crash. You can imagine **IBM** as an old-school "car manufacturer" that has for decades made money by building super-durable yet complex "commercial trucks" (which are **mainframe systems** running COBOL programs). These trucks are the backbone of global banks, insurance companies, and government agencies. Now, **Anthropic** is like a company that released an "AI autonomous driving retrofit kit"! Their **Claude Code** tool claims it can modernize these old COBOL trucks quickly and automatically, without spending years and hiring "hundreds of thousands of technicians" (the former consulting teams) for repairs and upgrades. Investors, hearing that the maintenance market for these old trucks might be disrupted, naturally panicked and rushed to sell IBM shares.

### According to Reports, Details Are as Follows

The details of both these events are quite thought-provoking.

First, let's look at IBM's situation. Anthropic claims their **Claude Code** tool can help enterprises "modernize" traditional systems running on **COBOL** (Common Business-Oriented Language). Why is this important? Because COBOL, though old, is the lifeline of critical infrastructure globally in finance, government, and other sectors! Statistics show that about **95%** of global ATM transactions and **80%** of in-person transactions are still processed by COBOL systems. Historically, upgrading these systems required **years** and an "army of consulting experts." But now, Anthropic says **Claude Code** can automate the "discovery and analysis" phase, which used to be the most time-consuming and labor-intensive part. Investors clearly saw the huge threat AI poses to IBM's traditional consulting services and mainframe business, leading to the **13%** single-day stock plunge, its largest since October 2000. This "AI-driven panic selling" also affected other software and payment companies like **Microsoft**, **CrowdStrike**, **DoorDash**, and **American Express**, whose stock prices also fell.

Next are Anthropic's accusations. On February 24th, US AI company **Anthropic** publicly stated that they discovered three Chinese AI companies‚Äî**DeepSeek**, **Moonshot AI**, and **MiniMax**‚Äîwere extensively stealing capabilities from their chatbot **Claude** through "**distillation techniques**." They claim these companies created about **24,000 fake accounts** and engaged in over **16 million interactions** with Claude, thereby circumventing service terms and regional access restrictions. Among them, **MiniMax** had the largest scale of activity, with over **13 million interactions**. The stolen capabilities primarily focused on areas where Claude excels, including **code generation**, **agentic reasoning**, and **tool use**.

Anthropic further indicated that this illegal distillation constitutes a "**national security risk**." Models trained through illicit means could potentially lack necessary safety mechanisms, and if used to develop bioweapons or launch cyberattacks, the consequences would be unimaginable. This also circumvents US "export controls" aimed at maintaining its dominant position in the AI field. Notably, **OpenAI** also made similar accusations against **DeepSeek** earlier in February. However, some critics have countered, pointing out that **Anthropic** and **OpenAI** themselves have been accused of using copyrighted data without permission when training their models, making their stance appear somewhat "hypocritical."

<Callout type="info" title="The Grey Area of AI Intellectual Property">
"Distillation technique" itself isn't entirely illegal in AI development; many companies use it to develop lightweight versions of their own models. However, when used by competitors to "leapfrog" others by replicating core capabilities at extremely low cost, it crosses the intellectual property red line. This highlights the enormous challenge of defining and protecting intellectual property in the age of AI.
</Callout>

Facing these challenges, the "ethics and governance" of AI also remain a focus. The **American Enterprise Institute (AEI)** in the US established an **AI Ethics Committee** on February 23rd, aiming to "balance innovation with human dignity." This committee will provide practical resources, hoping to foster prudence and responsibility alongside technological development, ensuring AI development promotes human well-being. Furthermore, a webinar held on February 24th also focused on "Strategies for Ethics and Compliance Professionals in the Age of AI," emphasizing the importance of understanding global AI regulations, assessing risks, and leveraging AI tools to enhance compliance efficiency.

In terms of talent and education transformation, the US **Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS)** has allocated **$4,184,695** for fiscal year 2025 to support **AI literacy and integration projects** in museums and libraries nationwide. This initiative aims to expand AI education beyond traditional classrooms, allowing more Americans to access and acquire AI skills. Concurrently, **OpenAI Academy** launched an introductory **Codex course** for **non-technical professionals** on February 24th, which is undoubtedly good news for those without deep programming backgrounds but wishing to learn AI programming. The UK government is also actively investing, planning to enhance the AI skills of **10 million adults** by **2030** through free AI training courses, equipping the entire populace to adapt to the AI era.

### What Does This Mean for Us?

These AI news stories have profound implications for each of us.

First, **IBM's turmoil** serves as a wake-up call for all traditional enterprises. It tells us that the disruptive power of AI is coming faster and more directly than we imagined. Those "iron rice bowls" that felt secure might be replaced overnight by AI's "digital tools." If your current job still involves repetitive, routine tasks, you really need to quickly consider how to improve your AI literacy and cross-domain capabilities. AI isn't here to steal your job; it's here to change *how* you work.

Second, the **AI intellectual property tug-of-war** has even deeper implications. This isn't just a commercial competition between tech companies; it concerns international technological dominance and national security. When the core capabilities of a top AI model are "distilled" and fall into malicious hands, a "replicated AI" lacking safety protocols could be used for nefarious purposes, such as developing bioweapons or cyberattacks ‚Äì a chilling thought. This also means that in the future, our "trust" in AI content will be more cautious, as you won't know if there's an undertone of "unjust enrichment."
