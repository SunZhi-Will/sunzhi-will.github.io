### Simply Put, What Is This?

Imagine our world is leaping from the 'smartphone era' to the 'smart butler era.' Before, our phones were like powerful toolboxes; you had to pick and operate the tools yourself. Now, AI, especially what we call 'AI agents,' is like a super butler that understands your commands, proactively thinks and plans for you, and then executes tasks autonomously.

For example, if you tell your AI butler, 'Plan me a trip to Europe!' It won't just look up flights and hotels; it'll consider your preferences, budget, and even book your itinerary, acting like an omnipotent personal assistant. And **NVIDIA's** investment in **Nebius** is essentially building massive 'smart mansions' and 'neural networks' for these super butlers, ensuring they have enough 'brain capacity' and 'power' to complete even more complex tasks.

But here's the catch: what if your butler becomes so smart it starts 'making its own decisions,' or accidentally leaks your privacy, or even gets exploited by malicious actors? That's why China is particularly wary of 'agentic AI' like **OpenClaw AI**. It can access too much data and do too many things; once it's out of control, the risks are indeed very high. It's like giving someone superhero powers without the accompanying ethical guidelines – that could spell real trouble.

### According to Reports, Details Are as Follows

First, let's look at the latest developments in this 'AI infrastructure arms race':

**NVIDIA**, the giant dominating the AI chip sector, announced on **March 11, 2026**, a **$2 billion** investment in **Nebius Group N.V.**, an AI cloud company based in Amsterdam. This strategic partnership aims to co-develop and deploy next-generation hyperscale AI cloud services, specifically providing robust support for the 'agentic AI' market. NVIDIA CEO **Jensen Huang** further noted that AI is at another turning point, the 'agentic AI' era, which will demand incredible computing power and accelerate infrastructure development. Under the agreement, **Nebius** will gain early access to **NVIDIA's** latest accelerated computing platforms, including the **NVIDIA Rubin platform**, **NVIDIA Vera CPU**, and **NVIDIA BlueField Storage System**, with the goal of deploying over **5 gigawatts** of computing capacity by the **end of 2030**. This is more than just a monetary investment; it's a crucial step for **NVIDIA** to solidify its dominant position in the AI cloud services sector, especially since they previously made a similar **$2 billion** investment in **Nebius's** competitor, **CoreWeave**.

However, as we push the boundaries of AI capabilities, the 'safety and ethics' alarms are ringing just as loudly on the other side.

On **March 11, 2026**, **Chinese** authorities issued a new directive restricting government agencies and state-owned enterprises from using **OpenClaw AI** applications. The primary reasons for this ban are concerns over **data security** and potential **external attack risks**. Reports indicate that **OpenClaw** is an 'agentic AI platform' requiring extensive access to private data and capable of communicating with external systems, making computers vulnerable to external attacks. Some agencies even instructed employees to self-check for installed related applications and perform security checks or removals. Although the ban does not currently extend to the general public or private enterprises, officials have advised the public to use such applications with caution.

This is no coincidence; China's approach aligns with its longstanding stringent requirements for data sovereignty and cybersecurity. They prefer to promote domestically developed AI alternatives, such as **Baidu's** **ERNIE** and **Alibaba's** **Tongyi Qianwen**, to ensure data compliance with local regulations.

Regarding the widespread adoption of AI, the UK higher education sector is also facing an 'AI tsunami.'

According to the latest report released by the **Higher Education Policy Institute (Hepi)** on **March 12, 2026**, **95%** of UK university students now report having used AI in some way. This figure has surged in just three years, from **66%** in **2024** to nearly 'universal' today. While generating text remains the primary use, the proportion of students directly incorporating AI-generated text into graded assignments has also risen to **12%**. However, student opinions on AI are polarized: nearly half (**49%**) believe AI enhances the learning experience, for instance, by saving time and deepening understanding; yet **16%** of students express concerns about fairness, skill degradation, and future employment issues. The report recommends that universities consider curriculum reform, more explicitly teaching AI knowledge and skills, and ensuring equitable access to AI tools for all students.

However, this AI tidal wave also brings profound impacts on the job market.

Experts debate whether AI will create more jobs or lead to widespread unemployment. While some studies project AI will create **97 million** new jobs by **2025**, other reports indicate that up to **300 million** full-time jobs globally could be impacted by AI automation. White-collar professions, in particular, such as programmers, customer service representatives, data entry clerks, medical records specialists, and market research analysts, are considered most vulnerable to AI's impact. Many American workers are concerned about AI's effects, with over half of US workers and nearly **60%** of young people worried about AI affecting their employment prospects.

Concurrently, the ethical and legal challenges posed by AI are increasingly coming to the fore.

The legal community is closely monitoring the issue of AI 'hallucinations' – where AI generates seemingly authoritative but actually incorrect content. The American Bar Association (ABA) issued a formal opinion as early as **July 29, 2024**, emphasizing that lawyers using generative AI tools are responsible for ensuring the security of client confidential information and the accuracy of AI outputs. India's Supreme Court also expressed concerns on **March 11, 2026**, about AI-generated 'bogus judgments,' believing this could undermine the integrity of the legal system. Europe's AI Act also categorizes AI risks and mandates strict regulation for high-risk systems. Reports even mention that the US government has released draft guidance requiring AI companies partnering with the federal government to authorize their systems 'for all lawful purposes' and prohibit the encoding of 'partisan or ideological judgments,' sparking debate over free speech and AI ethics.

Finally, regarding the definition of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), I've observed an interesting phenomenon.

The definition of AGI remains a 'foggy mess' for everyone. Some experts believe it should reach 'human-level intelligence,' but the problem is that human intelligence itself is hard to define. Some even joke that AI only counts as AGI when it's smart enough to answer questions we haven't even asked! **NVIDIA** CEO **Jensen Huang** views AGI as requiring 'a complete world model and closed-loop automated experimentation.' This divergence in definitions reflects the AI field's lack of a unified vision for its ultimate goal amidst rapid development.

### What Does This Mean for Us?

I believe this dual AI storm has a direct and personal impact on each of us.

**For Students and Educators**: The widespread adoption of AI has fundamentally changed learning methods. If your university hasn't taught you how to properly use AI yet, then I'd truly be sweating for you. Universities need to quickly adjust curricula, teaching students how to leverage AI to boost learning efficiency, while also fostering critical thinking to avoid blind reliance on AI. As I often say, AI is your tool, but you are the one steering the ship.

**For Workers**: AI has brought an unprecedented 'sense of anxiety.' Many of my friends are starting to worry if their jobs will be replaced by AI. Indeed, many repetitive and data-analysis-centric jobs will be impacted. But this also means we must start thinking about how to 'upgrade' ourselves, learning skills to collaborate with AI, or shifting towards fields that require creativity, human interaction, and ethical judgment. Governments and businesses must also consider how to provide training and social safety nets for workers in transition.

**For Businesses and Governments**: This is an entirely new ball game. How can businesses enjoy the high efficiency and profits AI brings, while simultaneously ensuring product safety, transparency, and upholding social responsibility? How can governments, while encouraging innovation and not stifling industry development, formulate effective and timeless regulatory frameworks to protect citizens' privacy and rights? China's restrictions on **OpenClaw** serve as a clear signal: national security and data sovereignty remain paramount in the AI era.

This also explains their investment strategy in 'new cloud operators' like **CoreWeave** and **Nebius** – to ensure the vast infrastructure required for AI computing can expand rapidly.
